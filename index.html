Tax Debts in Bankruptcy

A plain-language guide to discharging IRS and state tax debts in bankruptcy. Learn the 3-year, 2-year, and 240-day rules that determine whether your income tax debt can be wiped out under Chapter 7 or Chapter 13.

Can You Discharge Tax Debt in Bankruptcy?

Yes, sometimes. This surprises most people, but certain income tax debts can be discharged in bankruptcy -- meaning you no longer owe them after your case is complete. The key word is "certain." Not all taxes qualify, and the rules are strict.

Federal and state income taxes can become dischargeable if they meet three specific timing requirements. These rules exist because Congress decided that older tax debts -- where the government had years to collect but did not -- should be treated more like ordinary unsecured debt.

The analysis matters because if your taxes are dischargeable, bankruptcy may be the most effective tool available to resolve them. If they are not dischargeable, filing bankruptcy could still help by allowing you to pay the taxes over time through a Chapter 13 plan while stopping penalties and collection activity.

The Three Rules for Discharging Income Taxes

All three of these rules must be satisfied for an income tax debt to be dischargeable. If any one fails, the tax survives bankruptcy.

Rule 1: The 3-Year Rule (11 U.S.C. Section 507(a)(8)(A)(i))

The tax return for the debt must have been due at least 3 years before the bankruptcy filing date. This includes any extensions. For example, a 2022 tax return was due April 15, 2023 (or October 15, 2023 with extension). That return would meet the 3-year rule no earlier than April 15, 2026 (or October 15, 2026 if extended).

Rule 2: The 2-Year Rule (11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)(1)(B))

The tax return must have been actually filed at least 2 years before the bankruptcy petition date. If you filed a late return on March 1, 2024, this rule is not satisfied until March 1, 2026 -- regardless of when the return was originally due. Note: some courts hold that a return filed after the IRS makes a substitute assessment is not a "return" at all and can never satisfy this rule.

Rule 3: The 240-Day Rule (11 U.S.C. Section 507(a)(8)(A)(ii))

The tax must have been assessed by the IRS (or state tax authority) at least 240 days before the bankruptcy filing date. Assessment usually happens when you file your return, but it can be delayed by audits, amended returns, or tax court proceedings. Offers in compromise and prior bankruptcy filings can toll (pause) this 240-day clock.

You can request a tax transcript from the IRS (Form 4506-T) to find exact assessment dates. For state taxes, contact your state revenue department for assessment records.

What Taxes Are Never Dischargeable?

Even if the timing rules above are met, certain categories of tax debt can never be discharged in bankruptcy:

  • Payroll taxes (trust fund taxes) -- taxes you withheld from employee wages (FICA, income tax withholding). These are held in trust for the government and are never dischargeable under 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a)(1)(A).
  • Fraud penalties -- if the IRS assessed a fraud penalty, or if you filed a fraudulent return, the associated tax debt is nondischargeable under Section 523(a)(1)(C).
  • Tax evasion -- if you willfully attempted to evade or defeat a tax, that debt survives bankruptcy.
  • Unfiled returns -- if you never filed a return for the tax year, the debt is nondischargeable. You must file the return (and then wait 2 years) for the debt to even be considered dischargeable.
  • Recent tax years -- any tax year that does not yet meet all three timing rules remains priority debt and cannot be discharged.
  • Sales tax, excise tax, and customs duties -- these have separate rules and are generally nondischargeable.

For a broader overview of nondischargeable debts, see nondischargeable.org and 523a.org.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13 for Tax Debt

Chapter 7 can discharge qualifying income tax debts completely. If your taxes meet all three timing rules and none of the exceptions apply, a Chapter 7 discharge eliminates the personal liability. This is the fastest path -- most Chapter 7 cases take 3 to 4 months from filing to discharge.

Chapter 13 handles tax debt differently. Priority tax debts (those that do not meet the timing rules) must be paid in full through the repayment plan -- typically over 3 to 5 years, but without additional penalties or interest accruing. Non-priority tax debts that meet the timing rules are treated as general unsecured debt and may receive only a fraction of what is owed, with the remainder discharged at plan completion.

Chapter 13 can be strategic even if your taxes are dischargeable, because it also addresses other debts simultaneously and stops IRS levies, wage garnishments, and bank account seizures through the automatic stay.

Tax Liens Survive Bankruptcy

Important: A recorded federal tax lien survives bankruptcy even if the underlying tax debt is discharged. The personal obligation is eliminated, but the lien remains attached to any property you owned at the time of the bankruptcy filing.

This means the IRS can still enforce the lien against property you owned on the petition date -- including real estate, vehicles, and other assets. However, the IRS cannot pursue you personally for the debt, and the lien does not attach to property acquired after the bankruptcy filing.

If the IRS filed a Notice of Federal Tax Lien before your bankruptcy, you may need to address the lien separately -- through lien avoidance motions, negotiation with the IRS, or simply waiting for the lien to expire (generally 10 years from assessment). Planning around tax liens is one of the most important parts of a tax-focused bankruptcy strategy.

Common Mistakes

  • Filing too early. If you file bankruptcy before all three timing rules are satisfied, the tax debt will not be discharged. Running the calendar wrong by even one day can cost you. Always verify the exact due dates, filing dates, and assessment dates before filing.
  • Not filing returns before bankruptcy. Unfiled returns make the associated tax debt nondischargeable. Many people assume they can file bankruptcy to escape taxes they never reported. The opposite is true -- you must file the returns first, then wait at least 2 years.
  • Ignoring state taxes. Most states follow rules similar to the federal 3-year/2-year/240-day framework, but not all. Some states have additional requirements or shorter tolling periods. Check your state's rules separately.
  • Not checking the assessment date. The 240-day rule catches people who assume assessment happens when they file. Audits, amended returns, and substitute-for-return (SFR) assessments can push the assessment date much later than expected. Always get IRS transcripts to confirm.
  • Forgetting about tolling. The 240-day and 3-year clocks are paused (tolled) by certain events: prior bankruptcy filings, offers in compromise, collection due process hearings, and innocent spouse claims. If any of these apply, add the tolling time to your calculations.
  • Overlooking tax liens. Discharging the debt does not remove a recorded lien. If you have significant equity in property, the lien issue may need to be addressed as part of your bankruptcy strategy.
